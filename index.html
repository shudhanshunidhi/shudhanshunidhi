1. Words in Context (Vocabulary)
EASY POINTS▾
Core Trick
⚡ Never use your personal definition. The question says "as used in the passage" — context is everything. Cover the word, predict your own fill-in, then match to choices.
Method
1
Cover the word — read the sentence and predict a replacement
2
Match your prediction to choices — don't pick the "fanciest" word
3
Plug your choice back in — does it sound natural?
Wrong Answer Traps
Common Meaning TrapMost common definition of word — but passage uses it differently
Tone MismatchRight concept, wrong positive/negative charge
Synonym BaitSynonym that fits another context, not this sentence
Related WordThematically related but changes the meaning
2. Text Structure & Purpose
MEDIUM▾
Core Trick
⚡ PURPOSE = why the author wrote it (argue/describe/analyze). STRUCTURE = how it's organized (problem-solution, claim-evidence). For underlined sentence: what JOB does it do?
Structure Patterns
PatternSignal Words
Claim → EvidenceStudies show / For example / In fact
Problem → SolutionHowever / To address this / One approach
Counterargument → RebuttalSome argue / Critics claim / Yet / Nevertheless
ComparisonSimilarly / In contrast / While / Unlike
Wrong Answer Traps
Too Broad"Discusses science" — misses actual purpose
Wrong Verb"Argues" vs "describes" — does author take a stance?
3. Tone, Attitude & Perspective
MEDIUM▾
Core Trick
⚡ Read adjectives and adverbs — they carry tone. "Alarming" = concerned. "Remarkable" = impressed. Map those words to answer choices.
Tone Word Bank
Passage SignalsCorrect Tone Word
Warnings, urgent language, "must," "danger"alarmed / concerned
Balanced, "on one hand... on the other"objective / measured
Questions mainstream view, pushes backskeptical / critical
Explores complexity, no clear verdictnuanced / analytical
Dry facts, no emotionneutral / detached
❌ Don't confuse subject's tone with author's tone. Author describing an angry character can still be calm and analytical.
4. Cross-Text Connections (Paired Passages)
HARDEST▾
Method
1
Summarize each text in 1 sentence: "Text 1 says ___ about ___"
2
For "how would A respond to B" → find A's position first, test each choice against it
3
Answers must be grounded in what's in the text — not your inference
Golden Rule
⚡ If Author A never mentioned the topic — you can't infer their response. Eliminate anything A would be neutral on. The answer must come from A's actual words.

5. Central Ideas & Details
EASY POINTS▾
⚡ Central idea = the ONE claim all paragraphs support. It's not first/last sentence. Ask: "What is everything in this text trying to prove?"
Detail Questions
❌ "According to the text" = go back and FIND the exact line. Never answer from memory.
Eliminate If...
Too SpecificOnly one paragraph supports it
Too BroadGoes beyond what the text actually says
6. Command of Evidence — Textual
MEDIUM▾
⚡ Underline the EXACT claim. Test each answer: does it DIRECTLY prove/disprove that specific claim? Related ≠ relevant. Partial match = wrong.
Illustrates (Literary) Questions
1
Quote must SHOW the claim — not summarize it
2
Specific concrete action > general statements
3
Two quotes both work? → Pick the one matching claim language most closely
Weakener Trick
Right answer creates DIRECT contradiction. If claim: "X causes Y" → weakener shows "X without Y" or "Y without X."
7. Command of Evidence — Quantitative (Charts)
MEDIUM▾
⚡ Read graph TITLE, AXES, LEGEND before data. 80% of wrong answers = misreading what the chart is actually measuring.
Graph Traps
TrapHow to Avoid
Correlation ≠ causationBoth went up ≠ one caused the other
Wrong time periodCheck x-axis range carefully
Wrong groupCheck legend — right line/bar?
% vs absolute numbersHigher % ≠ higher total if group sizes differ
8. Inferences
HARDEST▾
⚡ SAT inferences are TINY steps — almost directly stated. If an answer requires 2+ logical steps, it's wrong. Ask "Must this be true?" not "Could this be true?"
Wrong Answer Patterns
Too FarLogical but requires outside knowledge
Common SenseObvious in real life but text doesn't say it
Extreme Language"Always," "never" — text rarely supports absolutes
OppositePlausible but contradicts what text implies

Rhetorical Synthesis · Transitions
9. Rhetorical Synthesis
MEDIUM▾
⚡ Read the GOAL first. Then find the answer that does that specific thing — not just one that uses the notes accurately. Accurate ≠ correct for this purpose.
Goals & What to Look For
GoalRight Answer Has...
"Introduce to general audience"Simple language, broad framing, no jargon
"Emphasize a contrast"Contrast word (while/whereas/but) + two things
"Support with data"Specific number or stat from notes
"Explain cause-effect"Because/therefore linking two facts
10. Transitions
EASY POINTS▾
⚡ Ignore content. Ask: what is the LOGICAL RELATIONSHIP between sentence before and after the blank? Then pick the transition that matches that relationship.
Master Table
RelationshipTransition Words
AdditionFurthermore, Moreover, Additionally, Also
ContrastHowever, Nevertheless, Yet, Despite, In contrast
Cause & EffectTherefore, Thus, As a result, Consequently
ExampleFor example, For instance, Specifically
ConcessionAdmittedly, Of course, While it is true
EmphasisIndeed, In fact, Notably, Above all
SummaryIn short, Overall, Ultimately
❌ "However" in a sentence with NO contrast = WRONG. Match relationship first, word second.

Punctuation · Sentence Structure · Form/Function/Tense
11. Sentence Boundaries (Fragments & Run-ons)
MEDIUM▾
⚡ Two complete sentences cannot be joined with just a comma (comma splice). Need: period / semicolon / [comma + FANBOYS: For And Nor But Or Yet So].
Quick Fix Chart
SituationCorrect Fix
Two independent clausesPeriod / Semicolon / comma + FANBOYS
Dependent clause firstComma after the dependent clause
Long intro phraseComma after the phrase
12. Punctuation (Commas, Semicolons, Colons, Dashes)
HARDEST▾
The 4 Comma Rules That Matter
Use comma to...Pattern
Join independent clauses + FANBOYS..., but ...
Set off nonessential infoThe dog, which was old, ...
After intro phrase/clauseAfter eating, she left.
Separate list itemsred, blue, and green
❌ NO comma between subject and verb. "The dog, barked" = WRONG.
Semicolon · Colon · Dash Rules
MarkRule
Semicolon ( ; )Only between two INDEPENDENT clauses. Like a period.
Colon ( : )After independent clause → list, explanation, example
Dash ( — )Sets off extra info. Pair of dashes = pair of commas.
Essential vs Non-Essential Test
⚡ Remove the phrase. If sentence still makes sense AND meaning unchanged → non-essential → needs commas. If removing it breaks meaning → essential → NO commas.
13. Subject-Verb Agreement
MEDIUM▾
⚡ Cross out everything between subject and verb (prepositional phrases, relative clauses). Then check agreement with what remains.
Tricky Patterns
Subject TypeVerb Is...
Everyone, anyone, nobody, eachSingular
Neither...nor / Either...orMatches CLOSER subject
Collective noun (team, group)Usually singular
There is/are...Match verb to noun AFTER it
14. Pronoun Agreement & Reference
EASY POINTS▾
⚡ Every pronoun must have a clear, unambiguous antecedent. Can't immediately tell what "it" or "they" refers to? → Wrong sentence.
Key Rules
RuleExample
Singular antecedentThe company raised its prices (not their)
Everyone/anyone → singularEveryone must bring his or her ID
Ambiguous reference"When Tom met Sam, he was nervous" → who is "he"?
15. Verb Tense & Mood
MEDIUM▾
⚡ Find other verbs in passage — tense must be CONSISTENT unless time shift is signaled ("previously," "by the time," "since then").
Subjunctive Mood
After "suggest," "recommend," "require," "demand" → use BASE form.
"The doctor recommends that he take (not takes) medication."
16. Modifiers (Misplaced & Dangling)
HARDEST▾
⚡ Introductory phrase must be IMMEDIATELY followed by what it describes. Subject right after the comma = who/what the phrase is about.
❌ "Having studied all night, the test was easy." (test didn't study)
✓ "Having studied all night, she found the test easy."
17. Parallel Structure
EASY POINTS▾
⚡ Items in a list or comparison must be in the same grammatical form. Find the first item's form → make everything match.
❌ She likes running, swimming, and to cycle.
✓ She likes running, swimming, and cycling.
Correlatives: both X and Y / either X or Y / not only X but also Y → X and Y must be same form.

M1. Linear Equations (One Variable)
EASY POINTS▾
Core Tricks
⚡ If the equation looks complicated — plug in answer choices. Start with B or C (middle values) to narrow down faster.
⚡ For "no solution" → make both sides identical except constants (coefficients match, constants don't). For "infinite solutions" → make both sides identical entirely.
Word Problem Translation
WordMath Symbol
is / was / equals / gives=
more than / increased by+
less than / decreased by / fewer−
of / times / product×
per / for each / ratio÷
at least / minimum / no less than≥
at most / maximum / no more than≤
No Solution / Infinite Solutions Trick
ax + b = cx + d
No solution: a = c, but b ≠ d (parallel lines)
Infinite solutions: a = c AND b = d (same line)
M2. Linear Functions & Graphs (y = mx + b)
EASY POINTS▾
Key Formulas
Slope-Intercept
y = mx + b
Slope Formula
m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁)
Point-Slope
y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)
Standard Form
Ax + By = C
Graph Reading Tricks
⚡ Slope = rate of change. y-intercept = starting value (when x=0). Always check units on axes — slope has units of (y-unit / x-unit).
⚡ Parallel lines: same slope, different intercept. Perpendicular lines: slopes are negative reciprocals (m₁ × m₂ = −1).
Context Interpretation
If question asks...Find...
"Initial value / starting amount"y-intercept (b)
"Rate of change / per unit"slope (m)
"When does it reach zero?"x-intercept (set y=0, solve)
"When are they equal?"Set equations equal, solve for x
M3. Systems of Linear Equations
MEDIUM▾
3 Methods — When to Use Each
MethodUse When...
SubstitutionOne variable is already isolated (x = ... or y = ...)
EliminationCoefficients can be matched by multiplying — fastest for SAT
GraphingQuestion gives you graphs or asks for intersection point
Elimination Trick
⚡ If question asks for x + y (not x or y separately) — try adding or subtracting equations directly. You might get x + y without solving individually.
3x + 2y = 14
x + 2y = 6
Subtract → 2x = 8 → x = 4. Or add if signs work out.
Number of Solutions
One solutionLines intersect → different slopes
No solutionParallel lines → same slope, different intercepts
Infinite solutionsSame line → one equation is a multiple of the other
SAT TrickFor no solution: set up ratio of coefficients equal, constants unequal
M4. Linear Inequalities
MEDIUM▾
⚡ FLIP the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a NEGATIVE number. This is the #1 source of errors.
Graph Interpretation
On GraphMeans
Solid line≤ or ≥ (includes the line)
Dashed line< or > (excludes the line)
Shading above liney > or y ≥ (greater than)
Shading below liney < or y ≤ (less than)
⚡ For systems of inequalities on a graph — the solution is the overlapping shaded region. Test a point in that region to verify.

M5. Quadratic Equations & Parabolas
HARDEST▾
Key Formulas
Quadratic Formula
x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a
Discriminant
b² − 4ac
Vertex Form
y = a(x−h)² + k
Standard Form
y = ax² + bx + c
Discriminant Decision Tree
b²−4ac valueNumber of real solutions
Positive (> 0)2 real solutions (parabola crosses x-axis twice)
Zero (= 0)1 real solution (parabola touches x-axis once)
Negative (< 0)No real solutions (parabola doesn't cross x-axis)
Vertex & Parabola Tricks
⚡ Vertex x-coordinate = −b/2a. Vertex form y=a(x−h)²+k → vertex is (h, k). If a>0 → opens up (min). If a<0 → opens down (max).
⚡ Factoring shortcut: if ax²+bx+c → find two numbers that multiply to ac and add to b. Faster than quadratic formula for clean numbers.
FOIL & Factoring Patterns
Difference of squares
a²−b² = (a+b)(a−b)
Perfect square
(a+b)² = a²+2ab+b²
Sum of cubes
a³+b³ = (a+b)(a²−ab+b²)
Completing square
Add (b/2)² to both sides
M6. Functions — Notation, Transformations, Composition
MEDIUM▾
Core Tricks
⚡ f(x) just means "plug x in." f(3) = replace every x with 3. f(x+1) = replace every x with (x+1).
⚡ f(g(x)) = composite function. Work from inside out: evaluate g(x) first, then plug that result into f.
Transformation Rules
Change to f(x)Effect on Graph
f(x) + kShift UP by k units
f(x) − kShift DOWN by k units
f(x + h)Shift LEFT by h units (counterintuitive!)
f(x − h)Shift RIGHT by h units
−f(x)Reflect over x-axis
f(−x)Reflect over y-axis
af(x) where a>1Vertical stretch
Domain & Range Quick Rules
⚡ Domain = all valid inputs (x). Watch out for: division by zero (denominator ≠ 0), square root of negative (under root ≥ 0). Range = all possible outputs (y).

M8. Polynomials & Rational Expressions
HARDEST▾
Key Tricks
⚡ Remainder Theorem: when polynomial p(x) is divided by (x−a), the remainder = p(a). If (x−a) is a factor → p(a) = 0.
⚡ Number of solutions of a polynomial = its degree (counting multiplicity). Even multiplicity → graph touches but doesn't cross x-axis. Odd multiplicity → graph crosses.
Rational Expression Tricks
⚡ To simplify: factor numerator and denominator, then cancel common factors. Restrictions: values that make denominator = 0 are excluded from domain.
Polynomial Long Division
Divide (2x²+5x+3) by (x+1):
→ 2x²÷x = 2x → multiply back → subtract → repeat
→ Result: 2x+3 (no remainder → (x+1) is a factor)
📐 Problem Solving & Data Analysis ~15% of Math
Ratios · Percentages · Statistics · Probability · Data Interpretation
M9. Ratios, Proportions & Percentages
EASY POINTS▾
Key Formulas
Percent of
part = (%) × whole / 100
% Change
(new−old)/old × 100
Proportion
a/b = c/d → cross multiply
Unit Rate
quantity ÷ units
Tricks
⚡ "X% of Y" = multiply X/100 × Y. "X is what percent of Y?" = (X/Y) × 100. Don't mix them up.
⚡ Successive percentage changes: 20% increase then 20% decrease ≠ 0% change. Multiply: 1.2 × 0.8 = 0.96 = 4% net decrease.
⚠️ "Percent more than" ≠ "percent of." If A is 20% more than B: A = 1.2B. If A is 20% of B: A = 0.2B. Very different!
M10. Statistics (Mean, Median, Mode, Spread)
MEDIUM▾
Definitions
MeasureWhat It Is / Trick
MeanSum ÷ count. Sensitive to outliers.
MedianMiddle value when sorted. Resistant to outliers.
ModeMost frequent value
RangeMax − Min
Standard DeviationMeasures spread. Larger SD = more spread out.
IQRQ3 − Q1. Middle 50% spread.
SAT Tricks
⚡ If an outlier is added: mean shifts toward it, median barely moves. Questions often ask "which measure changes the most?"
⚡ "If mean of n numbers is M, sum = n × M." Use this to find a missing value: sum − known values = missing value.
M11. Probability & Counting
MEDIUM▾
Core Formulas
Basic Probability
P = favorable / total
Complement
P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
Independent Events
P(A and B) = P(A)×P(B)
Conditional
P(A|B) = P(A and B)/P(B)
Two-Way Tables
⚡ For conditional probability from a table: restrict to the given condition's row/column first, THEN find the favorable count from that restricted group. Don't use total row.
M12. Data Interpretation (Graphs, Scatterplots, Tables)
EASY POINTS▾
Scatterplot Tricks
⚡ Line of best fit: positive slope = positive correlation. Negative slope = negative correlation. Read y-intercept and slope as context values.
⚡ An outlier on a scatterplot is a point far from the line of best fit — not just an extreme x or y value.
Causation Warning
⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation. If a graph shows both variables increase together, that does NOT mean one causes the other. SAT always tests this.
Survey/Sample Questions
⚡ Results from a random sample can be generalized to the population it was drawn from. Results from a biased/voluntary sample cannot. Check the sample size and method.
