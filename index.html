1. Words in Context (Vocabulary)
EASY POINTS▾
The Core Trick
⚡ Never use your personal definition of the word. The question says "as used in the passage" — context is everything. The word probably has an unusual or secondary meaning.
Step-by-Step Method
1
Cover the word in the passage with your thumb
2
Re-read the sentence and predict your own fill-in word based on context
3
Match your prediction to the answer choices — don't look for the "most impressive" word
4
Plug your chosen answer back in to confirm it sounds natural
Wrong Answer Traps
Common Meaning TrapThe most common meaning of the word — but the passage uses it differently
Tone MismatchRight concept, but too positive/negative for the passage
Synonym BaitA synonym of the word in a different context — doesn't fit this sentence
Related WordFeels thematically relevant but changes the meaning
High-Frequency SAT Word Patterns
If context shows...Look for answers meaning...
A plan or movement growinggaining momentum / expanding
Someone who doubts or questionsskeptical / critical
A result that supports both sidesnuanced / complex
Something hard to categorizeambiguous / elusive
2. Text Structure & Purpose
MEDIUM▾
Question Types
"The main purpose of the text is to..." / "The underlined sentence primarily serves to..." / "The author uses X in order to..."
Core Tricks
⚡ PURPOSE questions = why the author wrote it (argue, describe, analyze, compare). STRUCTURE questions = how the text is organized (problem-solution, cause-effect, chronological, claim-evidence).
For underlined sentence questions: ask what job that sentence does. Does it introduce? Contradict? Give evidence? Summarize? The answer must match the function, not the content.
Common Structure Patterns on SAT
PatternSignal Words
Claim → EvidenceStudies show / For example / In fact
Problem → SolutionHowever / To address this / One approach
Counterargument → RebuttalSome argue / Critics claim / Yet / Nevertheless
ComparisonSimilarly / In contrast / While / Unlike
ChronologicalInitially / Eventually / Over time / By [year]
Wrong Answer Traps
Too Broad"Discusses science" — too vague, doesn't capture what the text actually does
Too SpecificFocuses on a detail, misses the overall purpose
Wrong Verb"Argues" vs "describes" — check if author takes a stance or just explains
Opposite toneSays "criticizes" when author is actually praising
3. Tone, Attitude & Perspective
MEDIUM▾
Core Trick
⚡ Read adjectives and adverbs in the passage — they carry the tone. If the author calls something "alarming," they're concerned. If they call it "remarkable," they're impressed. Map those words to the answer choices.
Tone Answer Word Bank
Passage SignalsCorrect Tone Word
Warnings, urgent language, "must," "danger"alarmed / concerned
Balanced, "on one hand... on the other"objective / measured
Questions the mainstream view, pushes backskeptical / critical
Praises, celebrates, admiresenthusiastic / admiring
Explores complexity, no clear verdictnuanced / analytical
Dry facts, no emotionneutral / detached
Trap to Avoid
❌ Don't confuse the subject's tone with the author's tone. If the author is describing an angry character, the author's tone might still be calm and analytical.
4. Cross-Text Connections (Paired Passages)
HARDEST▾
Question Types
"How would Author A respond to Author B's claim?" / "Both texts agree that..." / "Unlike Passage 1, Passage 2..."
Step-by-Step Method
1
After reading each text, write 1 sentence each: "Text 1 says ___ about ___" and "Text 2 says ___ about ___"
2
For "how would Author A respond" → find Author A's position first, then test each answer against that position
3
The answer must be grounded in what's actually in the text — not logical inference
4
For "both agree" → look for overlap only where both texts have explicit statements
Relationship Types
RelationshipWhat to look for
AgreeBoth make same claim, may use different evidence
DisagreeOne says X causes Y, other says X doesn't cause Y
Same topic, different focusOne is broad, one is narrow; or different time periods
One extends the otherText 2 adds nuance or an exception to Text 1's claim
Golden Rule
⚡ For "how would A respond to B" — the answer must be something A's text actually supports. If A never mentioned the topic, you can't infer the response. Eliminate anything A would be neutral on.
📊 Information & Ideas ~26% of test
Central Ideas · Details · Command of Evidence (Textual & Quantitative) · Inferences
5. Central Ideas & Details
EASY POINTS▾
Core Trick
⚡ The central idea is NOT the first sentence and NOT the last sentence — it's the claim that every paragraph supports. Ask: "What is the ONE thing all paragraphs are trying to prove?"
Method
1
Read the first and last sentence of each paragraph
2
Find what they all have in common — that's your central idea
3
Eliminate answers that are: too specific (only one paragraph), too broad (says more than text does), or only partially true
For Detail Questions
Detail questions have a specific answer in the text — go back and find it. Don't answer from memory. The right answer will be directly stated, not inferred.
❌ "According to the text" = find the exact line. Don't use logic — find the words.
6. Command of Evidence — Textual
MEDIUM▾
The 3 Question Subtypes
A) "Which finding supports the claim?"
B) "Which finding weakens/challenges the claim?"
C) "Which quote from the text best illustrates [claim]?"
Universal Trick
⚡ Underline the EXACT claim/conclusion. Then test each answer: does it DIRECTLY prove/disprove that specific claim? Related ≠ relevant. Partial match = wrong.
For "Illustrates" (Literary) Questions
1
The correct quote must SHOW the claim — not summarize it
2
Specific concrete action/dialogue > general statements
3
If two quotes both show it → pick the one that matches MORE CLOSELY to exact claim language
Weakening Trick
For weakener questions — the right answer creates a DIRECT contradiction. Not just "raises doubts" — it must show the opposite of the claim using the same variables. If the claim: "X causes Y," the weakener shows "X without Y" or "Y without X."
Wrong Answer Patterns
Partially CorrectMatches part of the claim but misses a key qualifier ("only," "most," "always")
Same Topic ≠ Same PointDiscusses the same subject but proves a different conclusion
Too VagueCould support many claims — not specific enough to prove this one
Direction ErrorActually supports when it should weaken, or vice versa
7. Command of Evidence — Quantitative (Charts/Graphs)
MEDIUM▾
Core Trick
⚡ Read the graph TITLE, AXES LABELS, and LEGEND before looking at data. 80% of wrong answers come from misreading what the chart is actually measuring.
Method
1
Read the claim in the question carefully — note exact variables mentioned
2
Check: does the graph even measure those variables? If not → eliminate answers that use that data
3
Find the specific data point the claim references
4
Pick the answer that uses data from the chart to prove/disprove the claim — not the passage text
Common Graph Tricks
TrapHow to Avoid It
Correlation ≠ causationChart shows both went up — doesn't mean one caused the other
Wrong time periodCheck the x-axis — are you reading the right year/range?
Wrong group/categoryCheck legend — are you reading the right line/bar?
Percentage vs absolute numbersHigher % ≠ higher total if group sizes differ
Special Case: "Completes the claim" questions
These give you a partial sentence and ask you to complete it using the graph. The correct answer = a specific number or comparison directly from the graph that logically finishes the sentence. Don't paraphrase — find the exact value.
8. Inferences
HARDEST▾
Core Trick
⚡ SAT inferences are SMALL steps — not logical leaps. The right answer is almost directly stated; it just needs 1 tiny connection. If an answer requires 2+ steps of reasoning, it's wrong.
Method
1
Find the specific part of the text the question references
2
Ask: "What must be true based on this?" — not "What could be true?"
3
Eliminate anything that goes beyond the text or requires outside knowledge
4
Pick the most conservative, closest inference
Wrong Answer Patterns
Too FarLogical but requires knowledge not in the passage
Common Sense TrapSeems obvious in real life but the text doesn't support it
Extreme Language"Always," "never," "only" — text rarely supports absolutes
Opposite InferencePlausible but actually contradicts what the text implies
✍️ Expression of Ideas ~20% of test
Rhetorical Synthesis · Transitions
9. Rhetorical Synthesis
MEDIUM▾
What This Is
You're given bullet-point notes (like a student's research notes). The question says: "The student wants to write a sentence that [accomplishes X]. Which choice best accomplishes this goal?"
Core Trick
⚡ The question tells you EXACTLY what the sentence needs to do. Read the goal first. Then find the answer that does that specific thing — not just uses the notes accurately.
Common Goals & What to Look For
GoalRight Answer Has...
"Introduce the topic to a general audience"Simple language, no jargon, broad framing
"Emphasize a contrast"Contrast word (while, whereas, but) + two different things
"Support the argument with data"Specific number or statistic from the notes
"Describe a similarity"Two things being compared with a common trait
"Explain a cause-effect relationship"Because/therefore language connecting two facts
Elimination Rule
❌ Eliminate answers that are factually accurate but don't fulfill the specific goal. The SAT doesn't reward "correct" — it rewards "correct for this purpose."
10. Transitions
EASY POINTS▾
Core Trick
⚡ Ignore the actual content first. Just ask: what is the LOGICAL RELATIONSHIP between sentence before and sentence after the blank? Then pick the transition that matches that relationship.
Transition Word Master Table
RelationshipTransition Words
Addition (same direction)Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally, Also, In addition
Contrast / ContradictionHowever, Nevertheless, Yet, Although, Despite, In contrast
Cause & EffectTherefore, Thus, As a result, Consequently, Hence
Example / IllustrationFor example, For instance, Specifically, In particular
ConcessionAdmittedly, Of course, While it is true that, Granted
Sequence / TimeFirst, Subsequently, Eventually, Meanwhile, Later
EmphasisIndeed, In fact, Notably, Above all
Summary / ConclusionIn short, Overall, Ultimately, In conclusion
2-Step Method
1
Read sentence before blank — summarize its direction in one word (positive/negative/result/example)
2
Read sentence after blank — does it continue, contradict, or explain? → match that to the right transition category
❌ Don't pick a transition just because it "sounds formal." "However" in a sentence with no contrast = WRONG.

Punctuation · Sentence Structure · Form/Function/Tense
11. Sentence Boundaries (Fragments, Run-ons, Comma Splices)
MEDIUM▾
Core Rules
A complete sentence = Subject + Verb + Complete Thought. Two complete sentences cannot be joined with just a comma (comma splice). They need: period, semicolon, or a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So).
Quick Decision Chart
SituationCorrect Fix
Two independent clausesPeriod / Semicolon / [comma + FANBOYS]
Independent + dependent clauseComma before subordinating conjunction (although, because, while...)
Dependent clause firstComma after the dependent clause
Long introductory phraseComma after the phrase
Trick
⚡ Test if a clause is independent by reading it alone. Does it make a complete thought? If yes = independent. Can't join two independents with just a comma.
12. Punctuation (Commas, Semicolons, Colons, Dashes)
HARDEST▾
Comma Rules (Only 4 That Matter on SAT)
Use comma to...Example pattern
Join independent clauses with FANBOYS..., but ...
Set off nonessential (extra) infoThe dog, which was old, ...
After intro phrase/clauseAfter eating, she left.
Separate items in a listred, blue, and green
❌ NO comma between subject and verb. "The dog, barked" = WRONG.
Semicolon, Colon, Dash Rules
MarkRule
Semicolon ( ; )Only between two INDEPENDENT clauses. Like a period. Never before a list.
Colon ( : )After an independent clause → introduces a list, explanation, or example
Dash ( — )Sets off extra info (like commas) but stronger. Pair of dashes = pair of commas.
Essential vs Non-Essential Test
⚡ Remove the phrase in question. If the sentence still makes sense AND the meaning doesn't change → it's non-essential → needs commas/dashes around it. If removing it breaks the meaning → essential → NO commas.
❌ The scientist, who discovered penicillin, was Fleming. (wrong — essential info)
✓ Fleming, who won the Nobel Prize, died in 1955. (right — extra info)
13. Subject-Verb Agreement
MEDIUM▾
Core Trick
⚡ Cross out everything between the subject and verb (prepositional phrases, relative clauses). Then check if subject and verb agree.
"The quality of the samples were tested." → Cross out "of the samples" → "The quality were tested" → WRONG → "was"
Tricky Subject Patterns
Subject TypeVerb Is...
Everyone, anyone, nobody, eachSingular (Everyone is...)
Neither...nor / Either...orMatches the CLOSER subject (Neither he nor they are...)
Collective noun (team, group, committee)Usually singular (The team is...)
Inverted sentences (There is/are...)Match verb to the noun AFTER it (There are many reasons...)
14. Pronoun Agreement & Reference
EASY POINTS▾
Core Tricks
⚡ Every pronoun must have a clear, unambiguous antecedent. If you can't immediately tell what "it" or "they" refers to → the sentence is wrong.
Key Rules
RuleExample
Singular antecedent → singular pronounThe company raised its prices (not their)
Plural antecedent → plural pronounThe students brought their books
Everyone/anyone/nobody → singularEveryone must bring his or her ID
Ambiguous reference → rewrite"When Tom met Sam, he was nervous" → who is "he"?
They vs It
On the SAT: use "they" for plural people/things. Use "it" for singular companies/organizations. "The government changed its policy" NOT "their policy."
15. Verb Tense, Mood & Form
MEDIUM▾
Core Trick
⚡ Find the other verbs in the passage — tense must be CONSISTENT unless there's a clear time shift signaled by words like "previously," "by the time," "since then."
Tense Patterns to Know
TenseWhen to Use
Simple past (studied)Action completed in past
Past perfect (had studied)Action completed BEFORE another past action
Simple present (studies)General facts, ongoing states
Present perfect (has studied)Started in past, continues to now
Subjunctive Mood (SAT Loves This)
After "if," "suggest," "recommend," "require," "demand" → use base form (no -s, no -ed).
"The doctor recommends that he take (not takes) medication."
16. Modifiers (Misplaced & Dangling)
HARDEST▾
Core Trick
⚡ An introductory phrase must be immediately followed by the thing it describes. The subject right after the comma = who/what the phrase is about.
Dangling Modifier Test
1
Find the introductory phrase (usually starts with -ing verb or "Having...")
2
Ask: who is doing this action?
3
Check if that person/thing is the subject right after the comma
4
If not → dangling modifier → the sentence is wrong
❌ "Having studied all night, the test was easy." (The test didn't study)
✓ "Having studied all night, she found the test easy."
17. Parallel Structure
EASY POINTS▾
Core Trick
⚡ Items in a list or comparison must be in the same grammatical form. Find the first item's form, then make everything else match.
Common Patterns
PatternCorrect Form
List with "and/or"noun, noun, and noun (NOT noun, noun, and verb)
Comparisonsmore X than Y (both X and Y same form)
Correlative conjunctionsboth X and Y / either X or Y / not only X but also Y
❌ She likes running, swimming, and to cycle.
✓ She likes running, swimming, and cycling.
